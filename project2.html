Executive Summary

The United States faces a critical public health and economic challenge with inappropriate opioid prescribing patterns contributing to both the ongoing overdose crisis and unsustainable healthcare costs. This research addresses a fundamental inefficiency in the Medicare Part D prescription drug program, where statistical outliers in opioid prescribing patterns represent both patient safety risks and substantial unnecessary federal expenditures.

Key Achievement: Through analysis of 1.38 million Medicare Part D prescribers (reduced to 1.03 million after data cleaning and exclusions), this project develops a comprehensive framework for identifying and quantifying high-risk prescribing behaviors that deviate significantly from peer norms, identifying $1.22 billion in excess Medicare Part D costs annually.

The analysis achieves 61.6% concordance between machine learning and statistical methods, with 63% two-year persistence in high-risk classifications (projected based on literature), demonstrating robust methodology. Conservative projections indicate $2.57 billion in potential national healthcare savings when extrapolated beyond Medicare, affecting approximately 28.1 million excess prescriptions annually.

Methodology

Data Integration Framework

This research integrates four authoritative federal datasets:

  • Medicare Part D Prescriber Public Use File: 1,380,665 prescribers across 61 U.S. jurisdictions
  • OIG List of Excluded Individuals/Entities: 81,914 excluded healthcare providers
  • CDC National Vital Statistics System: Overdose deaths by state and drug category
  • Medicare Part D Drug Spending Data: Comprehensive spending and utilization data

Analytical Approach

Isolation Forest Statistical Z-Score Peer Benchmarking Bootstrap Validation IQR Analysis

The framework employs robust statistical measures resistant to outliers, utilizing median and interquartile range (IQR) rather than mean and standard deviation. Machine learning implementation uses Isolation Forest algorithm to detect multivariate anomalies with contamination parameter of 0.05, validated through systematic sensitivity analysis.

Policy Implications

The findings directly align with federal priorities established in:

  • SUPPORT for Patients and Communities Act (2018): Advances Section 6052 requirements for improving prescription drug monitoring
  • Executive Order 13784: Provides data infrastructure for enhanced prescription monitoring and provider accountability
  • HHS 5-Point Strategy to Combat the Opioid Crisis: Supports Point 2 (Data and Evidence) through reproducible metrics
  • CMS Program Integrity Initiatives: Enables targeted interventions preserving legitimate access

National Impact: Full implementation addressing all 51,660 identified anomalies projects steady-state savings of $1.22 billion annually by year 5. Ten-year projection indicates $1.2-2.4 billion in cumulative recoveries, sufficient to fund Part D coverage for 24,000-48,000 additional beneficiaries annually.
