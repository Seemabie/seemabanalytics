Executive Summary

The Medicare Program Integrity Analytics System for Home Health & Hospice Services introduces the Composite Risk Score (CRS), a novel framework for detecting anomalous billing patterns among Medicare certified home health agencies. The system integrates quality metrics from CMS Home Health Compare, patient satisfaction data from HHCAHPS surveys, and clinical measures from Hospice Compare into unified risk assessments.

Key Achievement

Analysis of 12,112 Medicare certified providers across all 50 states identified 12 high risk entities (0.1%) exhibiting statistical anomalies consistent with improper billing practices. The system achieved 91.7% precision with 99.9% specificity and 25% concordance with Office of Inspector General enforcement records, validated through 2,000 bootstrap iterations and 5 fold cross validation achieving 91.2% average accuracy.

The methodology processed 469,402 quality measures across 6 federal datasets in 4.3 minutes. The Composite Risk Score framework integrates three weighted analytical dimensions: Statistical Analysis (40%), Machine Learning via Isolation Forest algorithms (30%), and Quality Indicators (30%). This multi method validation provides confidence that identified patterns represent genuine anomalies rather than methodological artifacts.

Note: This system identifies statistical anomalies warranting further investigation, not confirmed fraud or abuse. Actual improper payment determinations require clinical review and appropriate enforcement procedures.

Analytical Approach

The novel Composite Risk Score (CRS) integrates multiple detection methods through weighted ensemble:

  • Statistical Component (40%): Z score calculations identify providers exceeding 2 to 3 standard deviations from peer group means across quality, satisfaction, and billing metrics
  • Machine Learning Component (30%): Isolation Forest with 300 estimators and 0.02 contamination rate for multivariate anomaly detection (AUC ROC: 0.94)
  • Quality Indicators (30%): Direct quality and satisfaction ratings below 2.0 threshold on 5 point scale

Problem Context

Medicare home health and hospice services represent a significant component of federal healthcare expenditures. According to the Medicare Payment Advisory Commission, Medicare paid $16.1 billion for home health care in 2022, serving approximately 2.8 million beneficiaries through over 11,300 participating agencies.

Medicare Fee for Service improper payments reached $31.70 billion in FY 2024, representing 7.66% of program expenditures. The HHS Office of Inspector General reported $7.13 billion in expected recoveries through 1,548 criminal and civil enforcement actions and 3,234 program exclusions.

The National Health Care Fraud Takedown resulted in charges against 324 defendants for schemes involving over $14.6 billion. Recent prosecutions include a $17 million California hospice fraud case and an $87 million Fort Bend County hospice fraud scheme that operated from 2019 to 2025.

Conclusion

This research demonstrates that systematic application of ensemble machine learning methods to integrated federal healthcare datasets can identify providers exhibiting statistical anomalies consistent with improper billing patterns while maintaining high precision to minimize false positives.

The methodology achieved 91.7% precision with 99.9% specificity, validated through 25% concordance with OIG enforcement records and 91.2% cross validation accuracy. The Composite Risk Score framework integrates three weighted analytical dimensions into a unified risk assessment system, addressing a documented gap in existing detection approaches.

The system processed 12,112 Medicare certified home health agencies using 469,402 quality measures across 6 federal datasets in 4.3 minutes, demonstrating scalability for systematic analysis of the Medicare home health provider population.

Important: These findings identify statistical anomalies warranting further investigation, not confirmed fraud or abuse. Actual improper payment determinations require clinical review and appropriate enforcement procedures by authorized agencies.
